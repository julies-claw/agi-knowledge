# AI Labor Market Impact — The White-Collar Recession

Anthropic published the most detailed map yet of which jobs AI is actively performing vs. which it could perform. The gap between those two numbers defines the near-term disruption timeline.

## The Anthropic Report (Mar 2026)

**"Labor market impacts of AI: A new measure and early evidence"**
Authors: Maxim Massenkoff and Peter McCrory (Anthropic)

Key finding: AI can *theoretically* cover most tasks in:
- Business and finance
- Management
- Computer science and math
- Legal
- Office administration

But *actual adoption* (measured via real Claude usage data) is a fraction of what's feasible.

The gap: most organizations haven't deployed AI into these workflows yet. When they do, the impact will be large and rapid.

Fortune headline: **"A 'Great Recession for white-collar workers' is absolutely possible"** (Mar 6, 2026)

## What's Already Being Automated

From the data:
- **Software engineering**: biggest impact already visible. Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Cursor already changing how code is written. Junior engineer tasks being absorbed.
- **Legal document review**: contract negotiation, due diligence, discovery automation (Harvey AI, Clio) — at large law firms, AI is now doing substantive client-facing work
- **Data analysis**: SQL queries, Excel modeling, report writing
- **Content creation**: drafts, summaries, translations

## What's Not Being Automated (Yet)

- High-stakes decisions requiring human accountability
- Physical jobs (the bottleneck is robotics hardware, not AI)
- Novel creative work requiring human judgment and taste
- Work requiring deep, trust-based client relationships

## Goldman Sachs Forecast

Goldman's AI displacement forecast (Mar 2026): "a structural shift in labor and capital"
- Productivity gains accrue mostly to capital owners
- Labor's share of GDP may decline
- Retraining at scale is necessary but historically difficult to execute

## The Counter-Argument

Every major labor technology transition (electricity, computers, internet) ultimately created more jobs than it destroyed — but with a painful transition period.

The open question: is this transition faster than previous ones, reducing the time for labor markets to adapt?
